From PLATO to Dragon's Lairs

How 1960s educational software gave birth to interactive storytelling — and why it matters for AI-powered D&D.

PLATO: The First Networked Community

1960 — PLATO (Programmed Logic for Automatic Teaching Operations) launches at the University of Illinois. Built on the ILLIAC I supercomputer, it was designed for computer-aided instruction.
1969 — PLATO IV introduces plasma displays, touch panels, and real-time multiplayer capabilities. It becomes the world's first online community.
1972 — Paul Tenczar creates the TUTOR programming language, designed specifically for interactive educational content with branching logic.

The TUTOR Language: Branching Logic

TUTOR's key innovation was conditional branching — content that changed based on student responses:

unit next
at 1510
write What is the capital of France?
ans (Paris)
next next2
write Wrong. Try again.
next next

This simple pattern — present → evaluate → branch — is the DNA of every interactive system that followed. TUTOR supported:

  • Help loops — when students struggled, TUTOR could jump to remedial content and return
  • Retry counters (the n1 variable) — track attempts and escalate difficulty
  • Conditional jumps — different paths based on student performance
  • Adaptive pacing — the system could slow down or speed up per-student

MUDs: From PLATO to Dungeons

PLATO users created some of the earliest multiplayer games:

1975 — Empire becomes the first networked multiplayer game on PLATO.
1975 — Dungeons of Pergamon, Moria, and Oubliette bring D&D-style dungeon crawling to PLATO terminals. Players explore, fight monsters, and level up in real-time.
1978 — Inspired by PLATO games, Roy Trubshaw creates MUD1 at Essex University — the first MUD (Multi-User Dungeon). The genre name itself comes from D&D.
1980s — MUDs proliferate across ARPANET and university networks. The branching, stateful, multiplayer interaction model is born.
🔗 The Direct Lineage: PLATO → PLATO RPGs → MUD1 → MMORPGs (Ultima Online, EverQuest, World of Warcraft) → AI Dungeon Masters. Every multiplayer online game descends from PLATO's multiplayer architecture.

The Future: State Machines + AI

Traditional game engines use hardcoded branching. Our approach combines:

  1. Deterministic structure — PLATO-style state machines guarantee no dead-ends (fail-forward)
  2. AI narration — LLMs generate rich prose, dynamic NPC dialogue, and environmental descriptions
  3. Player agency — meaningful choices that actually change the story
  4. DM evolution — the graph itself can grow during play, adding new paths based on player creativity
